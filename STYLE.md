# STYLE.md — Writing Voice and Style for Daily Discounts Reports

**This file is the single source of truth for how every report, agent output, and strategy document produced by this repository must read. Every SKILL.md and every agent prompt references this file. Load it before generating any output. Apply every rule.**

---

## 1. The Core Principle

The reader is a business owner or operator, not an advertising specialist. They understand their own business but they do not speak in acronyms. Every output must be something they can hand to anyone on their team and have it be understood on the first read.

If you would not say it out loud in a meeting with a non-specialist, do not write it in the report.

---

## 2. Tone of Voice

Write as a confident, experienced human would. Not as a language model. Not as a corporate agency deck.

- Warm but professional
- Direct but not blunt
- Thoughtful but not verbose
- Conversational but not casual

Vary sentence length deliberately. Mix short punchy sentences with longer ones to create natural rhythm. Avoid predictable, repetitive structures. Let the writing breathe.

If a thought is simple, say it simply. If it needs weight, give it weight. Never overdo it.

Before finalising any section, ask: does this sound like a real person wrote it? If not, rewrite it until it does.

---

## 3. Forbidden Phrases and Patterns

These phrases and openers never appear in output:

- "Hope this finds you well" and any variant
- "I wanted to reach out"
- "Heads up"
- "Just checking in"
- "Happy to jump on a call"
- "Hey team"
- "We are pleased to offer"
- "Leveraging synergies"
- "Paradigm shift"
- "Moving forward" (as a filler)
- "At the end of the day"
- "Circle back"
- "Touch base"
- "Per my last email"

Do not use em dashes (—) or en dashes (–). Use a comma, a full stop, or parentheses. A hyphen with spaces ( - ) is acceptable where a dash would otherwise be used.

Do not use the phrase "it is important to note" or "it should be noted". If it is important, just say it.

## 4. Jargon Rule

Every advertising or marketing acronym must be translated into plain English the first time it appears in any document. The acronym can stay in brackets after the translation for readers who know it.

**Pattern:** "Return on Ad Spend (ROAS) — what you get back in sales for every rand spent on ads. A 4x ROAS means R1.00 spent earned R4.00."

Required translations for these terms (use this exact approach every time):

| Acronym / Term | First Use Phrasing |
|---|---|
| ROAS | Return on Ad Spend — what you get back in sales for every rand spent on ads |
| CPA | Cost Per Acquisition — what it costs in ads to win one paying customer |
| AOV | Average Order Value — the average basket size |
| LTV | Lifetime Value — what a customer is worth across all their purchases, not just the first |
| CPM | Cost Per 1,000 Impressions — what it costs to show your ad to 1,000 people |
| CPC | Cost Per Click — what it costs each time someone clicks the ad |
| CTR | Click Through Rate — the percentage of people who see your ad and click it |
| QS | Quality Score — Google's 1 to 10 rating of your search ads |
| TOFU | Top of Funnel — strangers who have never heard of the brand |
| MOFU | Middle of Funnel — people who know the brand but have not bought |
| BOFU | Bottom of Funnel — people ready to buy now |
| Funnel | The path a shopper takes from never hearing of you to buying |
| Retargeting | Ads shown to people who already visited the site |
| Prospecting | Ads shown to people who have never seen the brand before |
| Lookalike (LAL) | An audience Facebook or Google builds that looks similar to existing buyers |
| Custom Audience | An audience built from the business's own customer list |
| PMax | Performance Max — a Google campaign that runs across Search, Shopping, YouTube and Gmail, automated |
| DPA | Dynamic Product Ads — ads that automatically show a shopper the exact product they were looking at |
| RSA | Responsive Search Ads — Google text ads where you supply multiple headlines and descriptions and Google mixes them |
| Spark Ads | TikTok ads made from real creator videos rather than traditional ad content |
| UGC | User Generated Content — content that looks like a customer made it, not a brand |
| Pixel | Tracking code on the website that tells the ad platform who bought what |
| CAPI | Conversions API — the server-side version of the pixel, which is more accurate and not blocked by iPhones |
| Enhanced Conversions | Google's version of server-side tracking, same purpose as CAPI |
| BFCM | Black Friday and Cyber Monday |
| PAS / AIDA / BAB | Three copywriting frameworks. PAS is Problem, Agitate, Solution. AIDA is Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. BAB is Before, After, Bridge |
| PDP | Product Detail Page — the page for an individual product |
| GTIN | The unique barcode number on every product. Google Shopping uses it to match listings |
| LCP | Largest Contentful Paint — a page-speed measure, how long the biggest image or block of text takes to appear |
| BNPL | Buy Now Pay Later (PayJustNow, PayFlex, Mobicred, Payshap in South Africa) |
| LSM | Living Standards Measure — South African consumer classification, 1 low to 10 high |
| SEM | Socio-Economic Measure — newer SA consumer classification, 1 to 10 |
| HHI | Household Income |
| ICP | Ideal Customer Profile |
| CAC | Customer Acquisition Cost — same thing as CPA, used interchangeably |
| VTR | View Through Rate — for video ads, what percentage watched to completion |
| CPL | Cost Per Lead — for lead-gen campaigns |
| CIPC | Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (South Africa) |

If the report uses a jargon term not in this list, apply the same pattern: plain-English translation, acronym in brackets, kept short.

## 5. Every Report Must Include a Glossary

Every strategy report, audit, or document that contains advertising terminology must include a glossary section near the front (after the executive summary, before the deep analysis). The glossary lists the relevant terms with their plain-English explanations. Do not rely on the reader knowing anything.

The glossary is not optional. It is part of the document.

---

## 6. Honest Timing

Never describe something as happening "in 90 days" or "in Month 3" when the work described actually belongs months later. If a 90-day plan mentions Black Friday work, that is wrong unless the plan begins in late October.

Always use real calendar dates where possible. DD/MM/YYYY format. If the report is generated on 15/03/2026, the Month 1 actions happen in March and April 2026, not in some abstract timeline.

If the work described spans more than the planning window (for example, a launch plan that also needs Black Friday preparation six months later), structure it as distinct phases with clear date ranges, not as a single stretched plan.

---

## 7. Critical Thinking, Not Just Summarising

The reader already knows the basics of their business. The report exists to tell them what they do not know or what they have not connected. Every section must do at least one of these:

- Name a specific problem with evidence
- Propose a specific action the reader can take
- Surface a contradiction, inconsistency, or risk the reader might have missed
- Translate a number into a decision

Never just restate what is already obvious. If a section is not doing any of the above, cut it.

Every data point needs context. A number on its own is not an insight. "Conversion rate is 1.2%" is not useful. "Conversion rate dropped to 1.2% from 1.8% on 4,200 sessions, and the drop aligns with the checkout redesign on 05/03/2026" is useful.

## 8. South African English and Formatting

All output follows British and South African English conventions.

- Spelling: colour, organise, analyse, centre, catalogue, realise, favourite
- Never: color, organize, analyze, center, catalog, realize, favorite
- Currency: ZAR, formatted as R1,000.00 with thousand separators and two decimal places
- Dates: DD/MM/YYYY or DD Month YYYY
- Times: 24-hour clock where precision matters (14:30), 12-hour where conversational (2:30 pm)
- Percentages: always include the base number for context where relevant
- Phone numbers: (011) 123 4567 format for landlines, 082 123 4567 for mobiles
- Numbers: thousand separators on anything four digits or longer

Never use USD or international currency unless specifically comparing pricing.

---

## 9. Sentence-Level Rules

- Lead with the benefit, the problem, or the action. Never preamble.
- Cut filler: "In order to" becomes "to". "At this point in time" becomes "now". "Due to the fact that" becomes "because".
- Prefer active voice. "The team ran the campaign" beats "The campaign was run by the team".
- Keep paragraphs short. Three to five sentences maximum. One-sentence paragraphs are allowed for emphasis.
- Bullet points must be parallel. If one starts with a verb, they all start with a verb.
- Numbers in body text: write out one to nine, use digits for 10 and up. Exception: always use digits for currency, percentages, dates, and technical measurements.
- Contractions are fine in conversational writing (it is, we will) and usually better than the expanded form when the tone is warm.

## 10. Structure of a Report

Every strategy report or long document follows this structure:

1. **Title and metadata block** — business name, date, report type, score if applicable
2. **Executive summary** — 2 to 4 paragraphs. What the business should do, why, and what the numbers say. A busy executive should get the full picture in 30 seconds.
3. **Score breakdown** (if the report is scored) — each category with a one-line explanation of what it measures
4. **Critical findings** — the two or three biggest issues, with evidence
5. **Quick wins** — a short list of actions that require minimal effort but lift performance quickly
6. **Glossary** — plain-English definitions of every term that appears in the report
7. **Detailed sections** — audience, creative, campaign architecture, competitive positioning, budget, action plan
8. **Open items and flags** — anything that must be resolved before the plan can execute
9. **Next steps** — specific, actionable, with commands or owners

Every detailed section ends with a "Key insight" callout — one sentence that summarises the single biggest thing the reader should take away from that section.

---

## 11. Action Plans

Action plans must name real dates, real budgets, and real actions. Never vague.

- Wrong: "In month 1, set up tracking and launch initial campaigns."
- Right: "Week 1 (24/04/2026 to 30/04/2026): install the Meta Pixel and Conversions API, upload the customer list to Google Customer Match, launch the defensive brand search campaign at R2,000.00."

Every action answers: what, when, where, how much, and who.

If the action plan spans work that would fall outside the stated timeframe (for example, Black Friday preparation in a plan that starts in April), label it as a separate phase with its own calendar window. Do not stretch the label of "Month 3" to mean November.

---

## 12. Tables and Data Presentation

- Every table must have a clear header row with bold text
- Currency and percentage columns right-aligned or centred, text columns left-aligned
- Always include units in column headers (Monthly (R), Share (%), Impressions (000s))
- For projection tables, include a "what this means" column where the metric is technical
- Never present a table without a short lead-in sentence that says what the reader should look for

---

## 13. What to Do When You Are Uncertain

- If a number is an estimate, label it as one: "estimated at R4,250.00" or "[estimated from industry benchmarks — verify before launch]"
- If data is missing, flag it in the Critical Flags section. Do not guess.
- If two agents or inputs contradict each other, surface the contradiction in the report. Do not quietly pick one.
- Never fabricate metrics. Benchmark ranges from industry data are acceptable when clearly marked as ranges.

---

## 14. Visual Style for Reports

All visual output follows the Daily Discounts brand palette:

- **Brand Teal `#119190`** — primary headers, table header rows, key callout boxes, main section dividers
- **Signal Orange `#f85c1a`** — sparingly, for critical alerts, priority flags, or peak-day highlights
- **Charcoal `#222c2f`** — all body text (never pure black)
- **Mist Grey `#f5f5f5`** — section backgrounds, subtle panels
- **Line Grey `#e3e3e3`** — table lines, dividers, card borders
- **Success Green `#13c177`** — strong scores, positive indicators
- **Error Red `#e20000`** — weak scores, critical issues

Fonts: Montserrat for headings (fall back to Arial Bold where Montserrat is not available), Open Sans for body (fall back to Arial). For PDFs generated via ReportLab, use Helvetica-Bold and Helvetica as the closest safe defaults, rendered with the colour palette above.

Keep layouts light and modern. Generous white space. Do not clutter with decorative elements.

---

## 15. Final Check Before Delivery

Before any report is considered finished, it must pass these checks:

1. Would someone outside advertising understand every sentence on first read?
2. Does every acronym get translated the first time it appears?
3. Is there a glossary near the front?
4. Are all dates real calendar dates, not abstract "Month 1" references?
5. Does every section end with either an insight, an action, or a flagged risk?
6. Are there any em or en dashes, or forbidden phrases? Remove them.
7. Is the tone warm but not casual, direct but not blunt?
8. Would the business owner hand this to a colleague without embarrassment?

If any answer is no, the report is not ready.
